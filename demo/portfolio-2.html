giverr

giverr is a project that I created over a 3 month period.
I started with a simple goal: create an app to help users get rid of unwanted items.

Read More

Background

When I began this project the problem was to create an app that would assist users with decluttering their home of unwanted items, I immediately got excited about the project because I have found myself in the situation of needing to get rid of unwanted items that I had accumulated over the years countless times in my life and I knew that I wasn’t the only one.

I started at the root of the problem by asking why do people buy things that ultimately become unwanted? I dived into secondary research and Instantly found that people do not only hold on to unwanted things, but they also spend money on unwanted things due to the idea of an emotional connection.

Having this understanding along with countless hours of research, interviews, and user testing helped me reframe the idea to create an app that would allow users to feel good while getting rid of unwanted items. As the lone UX professional on this project, I acted as a UX Researcher, Information Architect, Illustrator, Visual Designer and Strategist for the project.

My Process

To find a solution I used the Design Thinking process:

  • Empathize with your users.
  • Define your user’s goals, needs, and pain points.
  • Ideate by creating concepts for innovative solutions.
  • Prototype to create potential solutions.
  • Test these solutions.
  • Implement the final solution.

Secondary Research

I knew I needed to better understand the problem space so I started off my research process with Secondary Research to see what others had already discovered. I was surprised to discover that Americans spend $1.2 trillion annually on nonessential goods—in other words, items they do not need. From there I knew I was on the right track.

Key Findings:

  • The $8 billion home organization industry has more than doubled in size since the early 2000’s—growing at a staggering rate of 10% each year
  • Americans spend more on shoes, jewelry, and watches ($100 billion) than on higher education
  • Americans donate 1.9% of their income to charitable causes (NCCS/ IRS ). While 6 billion people worldwide live on less than $13,000/year

Heuristic Analysis

In order to make my potential solution market competitive, I went on a hunt to find apps and products that offer similar solutions. I chose the top 3 apps that attempted to alleviate these pain points which were Trashnothing, Freebie Alerts, and Free Stuff Listings. After conducting the analysis, I found that I was able to distinguish some things that will help me in the design process of my capstone project. After carefully reviewing 2/3 apps in full detail I notice trends in usability that did not seem pleasing to me, such as advertisements, prompts to sign up, and a lack of images to show what products are being offered.

Synthesizing the Results - Affinity Diagram

Once I wrote a detailed report for each users, combined with my initial secondary research, I was left with a large lump of data and insights within the subscription space. To sort these findings into logical groupings, I created an affinity diagram.

When I was creating the post-it notes to throw onto a blank wall, I started to discover patterns that guided my groupings in an organic way. These groupings were:

UX/UI Ideas

  • Give to charities
  • Use of app
  • Sell items first
  • Feelings
  • Moving

Through these patterns, the following insights were born:

  • Users are not fans of the Goodwill brand
  • Users often donate to charities
  • Users were frustrated with the use of classifieds apps
  • Users claimed an emotional attachment to unwanted items
  • Users feel good about donating to people in need

Synthesizing the Results - Empathy Mapping

As a way to establish empathy for my users, I created 5 empathy maps. I separated each map into 4 sections. The 4 main sections were:

  1. What users say.
  2. What users think and feel.
  3. What users need to see.
  4. What users do.

This mapping resulted in 2 type of users from which I created 2 personas.

Synthesizing the Results - Personas

Following my empathy mapping process, I established 2 personas which are based on all my previous research. Here are the 3 personas that I came up with:

  1. Kirsten “The Fashionista” Smith - Kirsten is a 28-year old independent social media manager who has gotten frustrated with the idea of trying to sell on classified apps like poshmark or offerup. She feels her clothes have value and although a buck would be nice, time is more important to Kirsten, she truly values her time off. The idea of giving to someone in need of her items makes her smile and is an ideal scenario for her. Her main concern is that the items do not end up in a Goodwill sold for half of what she paid for them while not contributing to the community.
  2. James “The Good Guy” Harper - James is a 33 year old teacher and student, he has accumulated lots of things of the years, and he knows he needs to get rid of some stuff soon. James is also a busy dad of 2. James has grown to have bad feelings towards the local Goodwill, but is on a time crunch due to life obligations. With immoral feelings towards the Goodwill brand, James is desperately seeking a church, a homeless shelter, or anywhere he can unload his unwanted items for a good cause.

Guerrilla Usability Testing

Since my designs could now function as a prototype, I uploaded my sketches to Marvel to create an interactive prototype.

Tasks

  1. How would you get to the profile section to edit your profile as an acceptor
  2. How would you donate an item

Insights:

  • 4 out of 5 users suggested for me to add the feature to notify the acceptor of the urgent item a giver may have.
  • 4 out of 5 users questioned what or why in regards to the features on the navigation menu.

Design Interface - Moodboard

Now that the technical layout of the app was completed, the exciting part of the process was started and the app began to establish an identity. I created a moodboard through Milanote in order to design the overall vision for the app.

I chose bright colors to steer away from the traditional stuffy colors of classified apps and to keep in line with my insights from the user interviews that users preferred bright aesthetics within their apps.

Design Interface - Style Guide

After creating a bright moodboard for the app, which at this point I had settled on the name giverr , I started building a style guide in Adobe Xd . This guide included the following:

  • Typography - PT Sans, and PT Serif Font that is universal for all iOS devices.
  • Color - A rich purple would be the main color with accents of bright blue and grey. Also, gradients were chose to follow modern UI trends.
  • UI Components - Buttons and shadows were all included in my Style Guide to create consistency throughout my app.

High Fidelity Mockups & Prototype

I began the much anticipated process of creating my high fidelity mockups in Adobe Xd at this point. I designed the screens based on the latest iOS iPhone screen size (896x414).

I chose to use Adobe Photoshop to create a friendly and inviting image as the login splash screen. My inspiration behind the image was the city skyline that gives the feeling of community. I carried the gradient background on the screen and within the app.

Throughout the app you'll see bright gradients contrasted with white space and purple. I wanted to retain as much white space as possible so the app wasn't cluttered.

From there I built a clickable prototype using InVision .

User Testing

Don Norman and Jakob Nielsen determined that the best number for Usability Testing is 5, so I scouted 5 people to test the InVision giverr Prototype. I did one unmoderated user test through TryMyUI and four moderated user tests.

Tasks:

  • Tell me how you would donate critical needed items to an acceptor(charity).
  • Manually add an item to give.
  • Find a local church to give golf clubs to.

Results:

  • 5 out of 5 users were stuck and did not know what to do after clicking the check notify icon.
  • 3 out of 5 users sat in silence for a second after I asked them to “proceed” at the splash screen.
  • 2 out of 5 users asked “what is that square icon at the bottom footer?” not knowing that it was the icon for chat.

Resdesign

I used the data from my first round of usability testing to guide my new designs for giverr.

Updates:

  • Added the “version” of the app to splash screen to clarify that the screen was the splash screen.
  • Added new menu icons to more recognizable icons.
  • Added gps icons on the map to show that the map is used to locate acceptors(charities).
  • Added a notification screen to notify the acceptor(charity) of a needed item a user has to give.
  • Added a thank you screen to give users the option to either get directions to acceptor(charity) location or call the acceptor(charity) for questions.
